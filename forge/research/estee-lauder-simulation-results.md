### Causes of the 30-Day Decline

The simulation identifies five compounding factors. None is sufficient alone; their interaction produces the observed magnitude.

**1. Iran War — Macro Contagion and Demand Destruction** *(initiating shock, Feb 25)*
War onset triggered simultaneous market panic (SIR contagion peak: 38% of market actors "infected" by Day 12), a 31% reduction in global trade volume via Strait of Hormuz and Red Sea disruptions, and a 42% energy cost spike. The Keynesian multiplier amplified these into sustained demand contraction. Consumer discretionary names like EL — already high-beta — bore disproportionate selling pressure as institutional portfolios rotated into defensives. EL underperformed the XLY consumer discretionary ETF by 25–31 percentage points over the period.

**2. Petrochemical Input Cost Inflation** *(cascade, Days 3–7)*
An underappreciated channel: 65–75% of EL's formulations rely on petrochemical-derived inputs — emollients (mineral oil, silicones), surfactants (SLS, PEG), fragrance bases (aromatic terpenes), and packaging (polyethylene, PET, polypropylene). A $25–30/bbl oil spike raises feedstock costs 15–25%. Combined with shipping rerouting via Cape of Good Hope (+10–14 days, +$1.5M/voyage), this represents an incremental $180–220M annual COGS headwind — **stacking directly on top of the confirmed $100M tariff headwind**, doubling the effective margin pressure vs. consensus estimates.

**3. US Tariff Escalation** *(crystallised at Q2 earnings, confirmed Days 7–18)*
US import tariffs rose from 2.4% (WTO average) toward 30% — a 12× increase — on beauty/cosmetics imports. EL management confirmed $100M H2 FY2026 gross margin impact on the Q2 earnings call. Analysts revised forward estimates downward. The regulatory shock model shows compliance cost peaking at 0.188 normalised units with a combined shock magnitude of 0.66, representing the stacked tariff and petrochemical impact.

**4. Structural Competitive Displacement** *(persistent, not 30-day specific)*
27% of US beauty consumers have purchased dupe products; the dupe market is growing 15–20% annually; MAC is among the top-10 most-duped brands. Masstige — the fastest-growing beauty segment at +14% — is capturing share from both mass and prestige. The Porter model shows industry profitability declining from 0.178 → 0.000 over the 30-day window as the weighted pressure of substitute threat (0.35), buyer power (0.25), and rivalry intensity (0.25) overwhelmed barrier protection. This is a secular shift, not a cyclical one.

**5. Puig Acquisition Announcement** *(single-session cliff event, March 23–24)*
The confirmation of merger talks with Puig (~€8.8B / $10.2B valuation; ~$40B combined EV) triggered a −10.1% single-session drop. The Puig shock was uniquely damaging because of its timing: it arrived when market contagion was mid-recovery (infected fraction falling from 0.382 peak toward 0.111) and sentiment had stabilised at a fragile floor of ~0.200. A new, binary, unresolvable risk (deal terms unknown; Puig family control; leverage concerns) was injected into a market with exhausted absorption capacity. 61.5% of luxury M&A destroys acquirer value at announcement. Analyst divergence had already hit maximum polarisation (1.0) by Day 3 — no consensus mechanism remained to defend the stock.

**Why the -10.1% single-day Puig drop was so severe:** The market had already priced in Iran war stress and tariff headwinds by Day 28. Sentiment was at 0.200 — a compressed floor that reflected a priced-in bad scenario. The Puig announcement did not worsen the existing macro picture; it added a **new, idiosyncratic, binary-outcome risk** (deal happens vs. doesn't; terms good vs. expensive) on top of the existing macro risk. The model captures this as an opinion reset event that pushes a system already at low sentiment into near-zero, with polarization already maxed — no bandwidth left for bulls to defend the stock.

## 4. The Iran War: Energy, Shipping, and Petrochemicals

This section explains the Iran war channel in detail, as it is the initiating shock that triggers the entire cascade. EL's exposure is not primarily as a geopolitical actor — it is as a manufacturer of petrochemical-derived consumer goods with global supply chains dependent on stable maritime routes.

### 4.1 Global Energy Costs

**Mechanism:** Iran controls the eastern shore of the Strait of Hormuz, through which approximately 21 million barrels of oil per day transits — roughly 20% of global oil trade. A declaration of war involving Iranian territorial waters immediately triggers insurance market repricing and tanker rerouting.

**Simulated impact:** `global__energy_cost`: 0.55 → 0.73 (Day 1), → 0.78 (Day 3). This represents a 42% increase in the energy cost index from baseline, corresponding approximately to WTI rising from ~$75/bbl to ~$106/bbl. Historically, Gulf conflict onset has produced $20–$35/bbl spikes within 72 hours; the model's 42% figure is at the high end of historical range, reflecting the broader Iran war context vs. localized incidents.

**Consumer impact:** Energy price increases of this magnitude translate to ~1.5% additional consumer inflation within 30–60 days. With US CPI already elevated and UoM Consumer Sentiment at 55.5 (2026 low), an additional energy-driven inflation shock is particularly damaging to discretionary spending. The Keynesian module captures this via `keynesian__fiscal_shock_pending` deteriorating on Day 1.

### 4.2 Shipping Disruptions

**Two concurrent disruption channels:**

**Channel 1 — Strait of Hormuz:** War-risk insurance premiums on Gulf route voyages triple or more. Shipping companies elect to reroute crude and refined petroleum products through alternative routes where possible, reducing effective tanker supply and increasing transit times. This raises freight costs on all goods carried alongside petroleum-related cargoes.

**Channel 2 — Red Sea / Suez:** Already disrupted by Houthi activity since 2024, the Iranian war escalates the threat environment further. Asia-to-Europe shipments that previously transited Suez now route via Cape of Good Hope. This adds approximately 10–14 days to transit time and $1.2–1.8 million per voyage in additional fuel and port costs.

**Direct EL exposure:** Estée Lauder sources manufacturing across three regions:
- Europe (France, Germany, Belgium): fragrance production, prestige skin care
- Asia (China, South Korea): mass-luxury and midtier product lines
- Americas (US): domestic skin care and makeup

Asia-to-Europe and Asia-to-US shipments for finished goods and components are directly affected by Suez/Red Sea disruption. EL's supply chain team is absorbing approximately $15–25 million in incremental logistics costs per quarter at current disruption levels — partially offsetting the $928M China revenue beat.

**Simulated impact:** `global__trade_volume`: 0.52 → 0.40 → 0.36. A 31% reduction from baseline. This feeds into SIR contagion (trade amplification) and Keynesian (below-0.5 trade threshold triggers continuous demand destruction). The trade volume stays suppressed through the entire 44-day window — no recovery modeled, consistent with no Iran ceasefire signal.

### 4.3 Petrochemical Industry Impact

This is the most underappreciated channel of the Iran war's effect on EL specifically.

**The supply chain:** Approximately 65–75% of beauty product formulations rely on petrochemical-derived ingredients. For EL specifically:

| Input Category | Petrochemical Basis | % of COGS | Iran War Impact |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Emollients (mineral oil, petrolatum, silicone polymers) | Crude oil → naphtha → ethylene → silicone chain | ~12% of formulation cost | +18–25% per war onset |
| Surfactants (SLS, PEG compounds, emulsifiers) | Ethylene oxide, propylene oxide | ~8% of formulation cost | +15–22% |
| Fragrance bases (aromatic terpenes, synthetic musks) | Benzene/toluene from oil refining; terpenoid precursors | ~15% of formulation cost | +20–30% |
| Packaging (polyethylene, PP, PET bottles, caps) | Ethylene, propylene from natural gas liquids | ~18% of total COGS | +12–18% |
| Solvents & preservatives (isopropanol, parabens) | Propylene chain | ~4% of formulation cost | +10–15% |

**Quantified exposure:** If petrochemical feedstock costs increase 20% (conservative for a $25/bbl oil spike), and these inputs represent approximately 35–40% of formulation COGS, the direct material cost increase is 20% × 38% ≈ 7.6% COGS inflation on affected product lines. For EL's H2 FY2026 gross profit of approximately $2.8B projected, this represents an incremental $180–220 million headwind — **stacking directly on top of the $100M confirmed tariff headwind**.

**The model captures this as:** `regulation__shock_magnitude` stepping from 0.28 → 0.46 at Day 3 (petrochemical cascade) and further to 0.60 at Day 7 (tariff crystallization). The combined shock magnitude of 0.60 drives `regulation__compliance_cost` peaking at 0.188 normalized units. With `cost_sensitivity=0.60`, the compliance cost represents approximately 11% of baseline margin — consistent with the combined $180–220M petrochemical + $100M tariff headwind on a ~$4B revenue H2.

**The adaptation curve:** The `adaptation_rate=0.08` models EL's supply chain response: renegotiating contracts with alternative suppliers (e.g., non-Gulf petrochemical producers in Southeast Asia or US), substituting synthetic silicones with plant-derived alternatives (squalane, shea butter derivatives), and adjusting packaging specifications. This takes 4–6 quarters and is why compliance cost falls from 0.188 → 0.043 by Day 30 in the model — not because costs disappear, but because EL is absorbing and adapting.

**Second-order effect — Puig strategic rationale:** The petrochemical exposure of skin care is significantly higher than for fragrance. Fragrance relies on aromatic and terpene chemistry that is partially substitutable with natural extracts. By acquiring Puig (a fragrance-heavy portfolio: Carolina Herrera, Rabanne, Jean Paul Gaultier, Charlotte Tilbury), EL is implicitly reducing its petrochemical input cost concentration risk — even if management has not articulated it this way publicly. The Schumpeter model captures this as the incumbent defending against both disruption (dupe culture) and input cost volatility through portfolio diversification.

## 6. Model Limitations

| Limitation | Impact | Direction |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Schumpeter timescale compressed at tick=day | Incumbent share collapses in days vs. years | Overstates speed, not direction |
| GDP floor at Day 10 — Keynesian too aggressive at daily ticks | Demand destruction modeled as acute vs. gradual | Overstates macro severity in 10-day window |
| Iran war duration modeled as persistent with no ceasefire | Trade volume stuck at 0.36 for full 44 days | Appropriate for base case; bull case requires recovery |
| No acquirer's discount module (GAP-01 identified) | Puig shock modeled via opinion reset, not financial mechanics | Directionally correct; misses deal premium quantification |
| No brand equity decay module (GAP-02 identified) | Dupe pressure enters via porter/schumpeter, not willingness-to-pay mechanics | Understates long-term price premium compression |
| Single deterministic run | No Monte Carlo uncertainty bands | Confidence intervals not reported |
